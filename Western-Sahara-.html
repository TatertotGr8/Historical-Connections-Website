Sands of Discord: 
Tracing the Roots of the Africa Western Sahara Conflict

Abtract 

The Western Sahara conflict is a dispute over who has control over the territory of Western Sahara in North Africa, and it has been going on for a long time. The conflict began when the Spanish colonized the area in the late 1800s, and it continued after many African countries gained independence in the mid-1900s. In 1975, the Madrid Accord ended Spanish colonial rule, but it also contributed to the ongoing conflict.
This project aims to study the historical connections between Spanish colonization, decolonization movements, the Madrid Accord, the Green March, and the involvement of the United Nations in the Western Sahara conflict. By looking at how the conflict has evolved over time, the project hopes to understand the underlying causes that have led to the conflict lasting for so long.
The project will also analyze the current political, economic, and humanitarian implications of the conflict. By doing this, the project hopes to increase understanding of the complex dynamics that are involved in the Western Sahara conflict and to contribute to efforts to find a solution that will last.

Spanish Colonization 

During the Colonial Era, Western Sahara was colonized by Spain. This period began in 1884 after the Berlin Conference, where European powers divided Africa among themselves, and Spain claimed Western Sahara as a colony. Spain established a colonial administration in Western Sahara and exploited its resources, particularly phosphate deposits and fisheries.

The Sahrawi people, who were indigenous to Western Sahara, resisted Spanish rule through various uprisings and protests. In 1957, the pro-independence group Polisario Front was formed, advocating for self-determination and  independence for Western Sahara. The Sahrawi people sought to reclaim their land and exercise their right to self-governance.

The Spanish colonization of Western Sahara lasted for several decades, during which the Sahrawi people experienced political, social, and economic changes as a result of Spanish rule. However, tensions between the Sahrawi people and the Spanish colonial administration, as well as the broader context of decolonization movements across Africa, eventually led to the end of Spanish colonization in Western Sahara in the mid-1970s.

Decolonization Movements 

 Decolonization movements refer to the widespread struggles for independence and self-determination that took place across Africa, Asia, and other regions during the 20th century, resulting in the end of colonial rule by European powers.

After the colonial era, during which European powers established colonies in various parts of the world, a desire for freedom and self-governance emerged among colonized peoples. This led to the rise of decolonization movements, which aimed to challenge and ultimately dismantle the colonial system.

Decolonization movements were diverse and multifaceted, taking different forms and strategies depending on the specific historical, political, and social contexts of each region. These movements often involved mass protests, strikes, civil disobedience, armed resistance, and diplomatic efforts to challenge colonial rule and demand independence.

The decolonization movements were driven by a range of factors, including nationalistic aspirations, desire for self-determination, rejection of foreign domination, socio-economic grievances, and the spread of ideas of democracy, human rights, and equality.

Over time, decolonization movements gained momentum and achieved significant successes. Many African and Asian countries gained independence from colonial powers, and new states were established. However, the process of decolonization was not always smooth, and it sometimes led to conflicts, political instability, and challenges in nation-building and governance in the post-colonial era.

The legacy of decolonization movements continues to shape the political, social, and cultural landscapes of many countries, as well as international relations, and has had a profound impact on the course of modern history. It remains an important and complex topic of study, reflecting the struggles, aspirations, and complexities of the process of decolonization in different parts of the world.

Madrid Accords

The Madrid Accords refer to a series of agreements signed in 1991 in Madrid, Spain, as part of the Middle East peace process. The main parties involved were Israel, represented by Prime Minister Yitzhak Shamir, and several Arab states, including Egypt, Jordan, Lebanon, and Syria, as well as the Palestinian Liberation Organization (PLO).

The Madrid Accords were significant because they marked the first time that Israel and its Arab neighbors engaged in direct, face-to-face negotiations to resolve their long-standing conflicts. The negotiations were mediated by the United States and the Soviet Union, and they focused on several key issues, including the status of the Palestinian territories, the borders of Israel, the rights of Palestinian refugees, and security arrangements in the region.

While the Madrid Accords did not result in a final peace agreement, they laid the groundwork for subsequent negotiations, such as the Oslo Accords in 1993 between Israel and the PLO, which led to the establishment of the Palestinian Authority. The Madrid Accords are seen as an important milestone in the ongoing efforts to achieve peace in the Middle East, although many of the issues discussed in the negotiations remain unresolved to this day.

Green March

The Green March, also known as the March of Return, was a historic event that occurred on November 6, 1975, when thousands of Moroccans, led by King Hassan II, marched into the disputed territory of Western Sahara. The territory was a Spanish colony at the time, and Morocco had long claimed it as part of its sovereign territory.

The Green March was organized as a peaceful demonstration, with Moroccans from all walks of life, including civilians and military personnel, participating in the march. The marchers carried Moroccan flags and chanted nationalist slogans as they crossed the border into Western Sahara. The event was widely supported within Morocco, and it aimed to assert Moroccan sovereignty over the disputed territory.

The Green March was a significant turning point in the conflict over Western Sahara, as it led to Spain's eventual withdrawal from the territory and paved the way for Morocco's annexation of Western Sahara.
