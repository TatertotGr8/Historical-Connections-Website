Colonialism and Decolonization

Colonialism is a phenomenon that has shaped the world's history, especially in the last few centuries. It involves the establishment of settlements or colonies in foreign territories by external powers and the subsequent exploitation of resources, labor, and land. All four events selected involve some aspect of colonialism and decolonization, which have had a significant impact on the regions involved

Mongol Invasion

In Korea, the Mongol invasion occurred in the late 13th century, during which the Mongol Empire attempted to conquer the Korean Peninsula. Despite initially resisting the Mongols, the Korean kingdom of Goryeo eventually succumbed to Mongol rule and became a vassal state. The Mongol invasion and subsequent rule significantly impacted Korean society, resulting in the introduction of new technologies and cultural practices (Cumings 2010).

The Spanish colonization of Western Sahara in the late 19th century was motivated by the desire to acquire resources and expand their empire. The Spanish established control over the region, with the indigenous Sahrawi population experiencing displacement and subjugation. The colonization of Western Sahara illustrates the extent to which external powers were willing to exploit other regions for their own benefit (Gough 2009).

However, in the decades that followed, decolonization movements emerged in each of these regions, reflecting a larger global trend towards independence and self-determination. In the case of Kievan Rus, the decline of the Mongol Empire and the emergence of new regional powers paved the way for the eventual independence of Ukraine. Similarly, Korea regained independence following Japan's defeat in World War II, which marked the end of Japanese colonial rule.

In the case of Western Sahara, the decolonization movement has been ongoing since the 1960s, with the Sahrawi people demanding the right to self-determination and independence from Morocco, which currently administers the region. The United Nations has been involved in facilitating negotiations between Morocco and the Sahrawi people, and while progress has been slow, the situation remains unresolved (Hodges 1983).

Geopolitical Tensions

Geopolitical tensions have shaped world events throughout history, often resulting in conflicts and wars. The modern era is no exception, as exemplified by the ongoing tensions between major powers in Russia & Ukraine and Korea. These events not only underscore the dangers of major power conflicts but also highlight the importance of diplomatic efforts in resolving such disputes.

Russia and Ukraine

To understand the tensions between Russia and Ukraine, it is necessary to examine the historical and political dynamics that have shaped this conflict. The roots of this conflict can be traced back to the Mongol invasion of Kievan Rus in the 13th century, which marked the beginning of a long period of foreign domination over the region. This domination continued with the rule of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, which lasted until the late 18th century, followed by the Russian Empire and the Soviet Union (Nimmo, 2015).


During World War II, Ukraine was occupied by Nazi Germany, and many Ukrainians fought alongside the Germans against the Soviet Union. Following the war, Ukraine was annexed by the Soviet Union and became one of its republics. Under Soviet rule, Ukraine suffered greatly, particularly during the Stalinist purges and the Holodomor, a man-made famine that killed millions of Ukrainians in the 1930s (Plokhy, 2014).


Following the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, Ukraine gained independence and became a sovereign state. However, Russia has remained involved in the region, particularly in the Crimean Peninsula, which has a predominantly Russian-speaking population. In 2014, Russia annexed Crimea, a move that was met with widespread condemnation from the international community (Bodner & Cullison, 2014). The annexation of Crimea has been seen as a violation of international law and has led to sanctions from the West against Russia.


The conflict between Russia and Ukraine is not just a political one, but also has social and cultural dimensions. Ukraine has a large ethnic Russian population, particularly in the east and the south, who speak Russian as their first language and identify with Russian culture. This has created a cultural divide between Ukraine's eastern and southern regions and the rest of the country, which has historically been more closely aligned with Europe (Plokhy, 2014).


Moreover, the conflict in Ukraine has highlighted the importance of economic factors. Following the annexation of Crimea, the Ukrainian economy has suffered greatly, particularly due to the loss of access to key resources and infrastructure in the region. Additionally, the imposition of economic sanctions by the West has had a significant impact on the Russian economy, particularly in the energy sector, which has led to a decline in living standards in Russia (Nimmo, 2015).


North Korea and U.S

The division of Korea into North and South following World War II was the result of a power struggle between the United States and the Soviet Union, which had occupied the northern and southern parts of the Korean peninsula, respectively. The division created a unique geopolitical situation, with the North under communist rule and the South aligned with the West (Cumings, 2010). This division has been a source of tension between North Korea and the United States, which has maintained a military presence in the region since the end of the Korean War in 1953.

The tensions between North Korea and the United States have been fueled by a number of factors. The North Korean government views the United States as a threat to its national security and has used its nuclear weapons program as a means of deterrence (Kim & Park, 2019). The United States, on the other hand, has sought to contain North Korea's nuclear program through economic sanctions and military threats (Kronstadt, 2019). These tensions have had significant social, economic, and political impacts on the region.

One of the most significant impacts of the tensions between North Korea and the United States has been the development of North Korea's nuclear weapons program. The North Korean government has pursued nuclear weapons as a means of deterring military action by the United States (Kim & Park, 2019). This has led to a number of international crises, including the 2017 standoff between North Korea and the United States over North Korea's nuclear program.

The tensions between North Korea and the United States have also had significant economic impacts on the region. The international community has imposed economic sanctions on North Korea in an attempt to pressure the government to abandon its nuclear program (Kronstadt, 2019). These sanctions have had a devastating impact on the North Korean economy, exacerbating an already dire humanitarian situation in the country (United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs, 2020). 




Chile 

The tension between Chile and the United States is a notable example of geopolitical tensions in Latin America that have had far-reaching consequences. The history of this conflict can be traced back to the 1970s when Chile was in the midst of a political crisis, culminating in the election of Salvador Allende as the first democratically elected socialist leader in Latin America in 1970 (Winn, 2004).

Allende's government implemented a series of socialist policies aimed at redistributing wealth and improving the lives of Chile's poor and working-class citizens. However, these policies faced fierce opposition from powerful domestic and international actors, including the United States government, which viewed Allende's government as a threat to American interests in the region (Kornbluh, 2013).

The United States government, under the administration of President Richard Nixon, actively supported the opposition to Allende's government and played a key role in the military coup that overthrew his government in 1973, bringing General Augusto Pinochet to power (Kornbluh, 2013). The coup resulted in widespread human rights abuses and political repression, with thousands of Chileans being killed, imprisoned, or disappearing during Pinochet's regime (Sikkink, 2011).

The role of external powers in shaping domestic politics is a common theme in geopolitical conflicts around the world. In the case of Chile, the United States government played a significant role in the overthrow of the democratically elected government of Salvador Allende and the establishment of a military dictatorship under General Augusto Pinochet. The U.S. government provided financial and logistical support to opposition groups, and U.S. intelligence agencies were involved in planning and executing the coup (Kornbluh, 2013).

This intervention by external powers highlights the challenges of promoting democracy and human rights in the face of authoritarian regimes. The United States government's support for the Pinochet regime resulted in widespread human rights abuses and political repression, which continued for over a decade. The Pinochet regime implemented policies that violated human rights, including torture, extrajudicial killings, and forced disappearances of political opponents (Sikkink, 2011).


The SPICE themes of political and social dynamics are also evident in the Chilean conflict. Political struggles for power and influence were a significant factor in the conflict, with the Allende government implementing socialist policies that were opposed by powerful domestic and international actors. Social dynamics, such as class and ethnic tensions, also contributed to the conflict. The Allende government's policies aimed at redistributing wealth and improving the lives of Chile's poor and working-class citizens were viewed as a threat by the country's elite and were met with opposition (Loveman, 1998).


Western Sahara Conflict  

Geopolitical tensions and conflicts are not limited to just Europe and Asia. The Western Sahara Conflict is an example of such a conflict in the African continent that has been ongoing for over 45 years. The conflict dates back to the Spanish colonization of Western Sahara in the late 19th century and the subsequent decolonization movements in the 20th century (Söderbaum & Taylor, 2008). After Spain withdrew from Western Sahara in 1975, the territory was claimed by both Morocco and Mauritania, which sparked a conflict with the Polisario Front, a national liberation movement representing the Sahrawi people, who seek self-determination and independence for Western Sahara.


The conflict escalated after Morocco's Green March in 1975, in which thousands of Moroccan civilians and soldiers crossed the border into Western Sahara and occupied the territory (Zunes, 2019). The United Nations intervened and brokered a ceasefire in 1991, which established a ceasefire line between Morocco and the Polisario Front and mandated a referendum for the self-determination of Western Sahara's people. However, the referendum has not been held to date due to disagreements over voter eligibility and the future status of Western Sahara.


The Western Sahara Conflict exemplifies the SPICE themes of social and political dynamics, as well as economic and environmental factors. The conflict has been driven by political struggles for power and control over resources, particularly the region's vast reserves of phosphates, which are crucial for the Moroccan economy (Bono & Santiso, 2008). The conflict has also had significant social and environmental impacts, including the displacement of Sahrawi refugees and the damage to the region's fragile ecosystem (McNeish, 2015).


The United Nations has been involved in various peacekeeping efforts and negotiations between the parties. However, a lasting resolution to the conflict remains elusive. As of May 2023, the situation in Western Sahara remains tense, and the parties have yet to agree on a way forward (United Nations, 2023). The Western Sahara Conflict is a stark reminder of the complexity of geopolitical tensions and the importance of understanding the underlying social, political, economic, and cultural factors that drive them.


To further expand on the social and political dynamics of the Western Sahara Conflict, it is essential to understand the historical context and the competing claims to the territory. Western Sahara was a Spanish colony until 1975, when Spain withdrew from the region. Morocco and Mauritania both claimed the territory, but the International Court of Justice (ICJ) ruled in favor of the right to self-determination of the Sahrawi people, who had formed the Polisario Front and fought for independence (ICJ, 1975).


Despite the ICJ ruling, Morocco annexed Western Sahara in 1975, leading to a decades-long conflict with the Polisario Front, which was backed by Algeria. The conflict has resulted in the displacement of thousands of Sahrawi refugees, who have been living in refugee camps in Algeria for decades (Zunes, 2019). The United Nations has been involved in various peacekeeping efforts and negotiations to resolve the conflict, but a lasting solution has yet to be reached.


In addition to the political struggles, the conflict has also had significant economic and environmental impacts. Western Sahara is home to vast reserves of phosphates, which are crucial for Morocco's economy. The exploitation of these resources by Morocco has been a contentious issue, with the Polisario Front and the Sahrawi people arguing that they have not received a fair share of the profits (Bono & Santiso, 2008). The conflict has also had environmental impacts, including the damage to the region's fragile ecosystem due to mining and the construction of a berm by Morocco to separate the portion of Western Sahara it controls from the Polisario Front-controlled areas (McNeish, 2015).


Cultural historical comparisons also provide insight into the Western Sahara Conflict. The conflict can be compared to other struggles for self-determination and territorial disputes, such as the conflict between Israel and Palestine, and the conflict in Kashmir between India and Pakistan. These comparisons highlight the complexities of territorial disputes and the importance of finding a lasting solution that takes into account the rights and aspirations of all parties involved.

Human Rights Abuses

The human rights abuses in Chile were not an isolated incident, and similar violations have occurred in other parts of the world, including in the Western Sahara conflict. This conflict began with the Spanish colonization of Western Sahara in the late 19th century and continued through the decolonization movements of the 20th century. The Madrid Accords in 1975 led to a peaceful transfer of power to Morocco and Mauritania, but the Sahrawi people were not given a say in the decision (Human Rights Watch, 2019). This led to the Green March, where thousands of Moroccans marched into Western Sahara to claim the territory for Morocco, resulting in the displacement of many Sahrawi people.

The Sahrawi people who remained in Western Sahara have been subjected to human rights abuses by the Moroccan government, including restrictions on freedom of expression and movement, arbitrary detention, and torture (Amnesty International, 2020). The conflict has also led to a large refugee crisis, with tens of thousands of Sahrawi people living in refugee camps in Algeria, where they face difficult living conditions and limited opportunities (UNHCR, 2022). The Western Sahara conflict, like the situation in Chile, highlights the importance of protecting human rights and ensuring accountability for those who commit abuses.

Culturally, they demonstrate the impact that government policies can have on the lives of individuals and communities.

A comparison of the two situations reveals some interesting parallels. Both involved colonial powers exerting control over a territory and people who were not given a say in the decision. However, the referendum has not been held, and the conflict between Morocco and the Sahrawi independence movement has continued.
